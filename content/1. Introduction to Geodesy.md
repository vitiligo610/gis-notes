### Geodesy
**Geodesy** is the study of the shape and size of the Earth. 

*   The circumference of the Earth is approximately **40,075 km**.
*   The Earth is **not a perfect sphere** due to topographic variations (mountains, valleys, etc.). However, relative to its massive size, the surface is considered quite smooth even when mountains are factored in.
*   **Oblate Spheroid / Ellipsoid:** The Earth is squashed at the poles. It is about 1/300th part shorter from pole to pole than it is across the equator. All standard measurements are based on this ellipsoid/oblate spheroid model.
    ![[Pasted image 20260427123824.png|507]]
*   **The Geoid:** In reality, the Earth is also "lumpy" due to variations in gravity. When considering these lumps along with the squishiness, the resulting complex shape is called a **Geoid**.

### Datums
While the **Ellipsoid** defines the *shape* and *size* of the Earth model, a **Datum** defines how that model is *anchored* or aligned to the real, lumpy Earth (the Geoid).

![[Pasted image 20260427125304.png]]

*   A datum provides the frame of reference for measuring locations, specifying the origin point, scale, and orientation of the coordinate system.
*   **Relationship to Ellipsoid:** Think of the ellipsoid as a blank, mathematical geometric shape. The datum acts as the instructions for exactly where and how to position that shape over the Earth's actual surface to get the best possible fit.
*   **Types of Datums:**
    *   **Global / Geocentric Datums:** Anchored to the Earth's center of mass. They are designed to fit the entire globe reasonably well but might not perfectly hug the local topography. *(Example: WGS 84, which is the standard used by modern GPS).*
    *   **Local Datums:** Anchored to a specific point on the surface to perfectly fit a specific region or country. They are highly accurate locally but wildly inaccurate outside that intended area. *(Examples: NAD 27 or NAD 83 for North America).*
*   **Importance in GIS:**
    *   Coordinates (latitude/longitude) are essentially meaningless unless you know which datum they are based on. 
    *   If you overlay two datasets in GIS that use different datums (without mathematically transforming one of them), the locations will not line up correctly. This misalignment is called a **Datum Shift**.
